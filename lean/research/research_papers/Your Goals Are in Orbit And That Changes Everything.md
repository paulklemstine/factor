## The Three Laws of Goal Dynamics

### Law 1: Goals Pull on Each Other

Isaac Newton showed that every mass attracts every other mass. In Orbital Goal Dynamics, every goal *influences* every other goal. Some goals are synergistic — exercising improves your sleep, which sharpens your thinking, which boosts your career. These goals *attract* each other, like planets in a stable orbit. Pursuing one creates momentum for the others.

But some goals conflict — working overtime steals time from relationships, which erodes your support network, which undermines your resilience at work. These goals *repel* each other. Pursuing one creates resistance for the others.

The crucial insight: **the interactions between goals often matter more than the goals themselves.** Choosing the right *combination* of goals is more important than optimizing how hard you work on each one. A set of synergistic goals can make you feel like you have a tailwind. A set of conflicting goals makes every step feel like walking through mud.

### Law 2: Momentum Is Real

A goal in motion tends to stay in motion. If you've been running every morning for six months, you have *momentum* — skipping a day feels wrong, and getting back on track is easy. If you've been meaning to start running for six months, you have no momentum — and starting requires an enormous force.

This isn't a metaphor. In our framework, each goal has a velocity — a rate of progress — and that velocity persists unless acted upon by a force. Sunk cost isn't always a fallacy; it's often genuine momentum that you've built. The question isn't "should I keep going?" but "is the momentum carrying me in the right direction?"

### Law 3: You Can't Speed Up Everything at Once

Here's the deepest insight, and it comes directly from the mathematics: **the total energy of your goal system is conserved.** You can redirect energy from one goal to another — like a spacecraft performing a gravity assist off Jupiter — but you cannot increase the total.

This is the fundamental constraint that makes goal planning hard. It's not that you lack willpower or discipline. It's that the laws of goal physics don't allow perpetual motion. To make progress on one goal, energy must come from somewhere — either from rest (starting a new effort), from another goal (reallocation), or from a synergistic coupling (where one goal's progress naturally feeds another's).

## The Magic Number: How Many Goals Is Too Many?

Psychologist George Miller famously identified "seven, plus or minus two" as the capacity of human working memory. Our framework predicts a similar magic number — but for an entirely different reason.

When you simulate a system of goals with gravitational-type couplings, something remarkable happens. Below a critical number of goals (call it N*), the system settles into stable, predictable orbits. Each goal makes steady progress. The trajectories are smooth and manageable.

But above N*, the system becomes *chaotic*. Goals interfere with each other in unpredictable ways. Small changes in effort allocation lead to wildly different outcomes. Planning becomes impossible because the system is inherently unpredictable beyond a short horizon.

Our simulations predict N* ≈ 7 for typical coupling strengths. Not because of memory limits, but because of *dynamical stability*. The math says: beyond seven active goals, you are trying to juggle in a hurricane.

**Practical takeaway:** If you have more than 7 active goals, you are past the phase transition. The solution isn't to try harder — it's to reduce. Put goals on pause. Combine synergistic goals. Drop conflicting ones. Get below N*, and planning becomes tractable again.

## The Gravity Assist: Steal Momentum from Your Own Success

NASA's Voyager spacecraft didn't have enough fuel to reach the outer planets on its own. Instead, it used *gravity assists* — flying past Jupiter to steal some of the planet's orbital energy, gaining speed for free. The planet barely noticed (it's enormous), but the spacecraft got a massive boost.

You can do the same thing with your goals.

The key is *sequencing*. If you arrange your goals so that completing one creates momentum for the next, the total progress is greater than if you pursued them in parallel. It's the difference between a chain reaction and scattered sparks.

Our "Gravity Assist Algorithm" computes the optimal sequence by chaining goals along their synergistic couplings. For example:

> **Design** → **Development** → **Testing** → **Documentation**
>
> Each phase's completion creates artifacts and understanding that propel the next phase. Doing them out of order (or in parallel) means each phase starts from scratch.

This is the mathematical basis for the productivity advice "eat the frog" (start with the hardest, most impactful goal) — but more nuanced. OGD says: start with the goal whose completion creates the most momentum for *other* goals. That's not always the hardest one.

## Resonance: Why Some Habits Reinforce Each Other

Astronomers know that Neptune and Pluto never collide even though their orbits cross, because they are locked in a 3:2 *resonance*: for every 3 orbits Neptune completes, Pluto completes exactly 2. This resonance stabilizes both orbits indefinitely.

Goals work the same way. When two goals are pursued at frequencies that form a simple ratio — you exercise every day and meditate every other day (2:1), or you have a date night weekly and a financial review monthly (4:1) — they enter *resonance*. The regular, predictable interaction reinforces both habits.

When goal frequencies are incommensurate — you exercise "when you feel like it" and meditate "sometimes" — there is no resonance. The goals drift in and out of phase, sometimes reinforcing and sometimes interfering. The net effect averages to zero.

**Practical takeaway:** Link your goals at simple, regular ratios. If you exercise every day, review your finances every week, and have a deep relationship conversation every month, you've set up a 30:4:1 resonance that stabilizes all three.

The productivity world calls this "habit stacking." We call it resonance. Same phenomenon, better math.

## The Optimal Friction

Here's a counterintuitive finding: **some friction is optimal.**

In physics, a damped harmonic oscillator reaches its target fastest at "critical damping" — not zero friction (which causes endless oscillation) and not high friction (which causes sluggish approach), but a precise intermediate value.

Our simulations confirm the same is true for goals. With zero accountability (zero friction), you oscillate — excited bursts of effort followed by complete collapse. With extreme accountability (high friction) — micromanagement, constant monitoring, anxiety — you crawl forward painfully slowly.

The optimal level of friction — the right amount of deadlines, accountability partners, and self-monitoring — produces convergence 2-3× faster than either extreme.

This explains why some structure helps (daily standup meetings, weekly reviews) while too much structure kills creativity and motivation. The math tells you: aim for critical damping. Enough structure to prevent oscillation, not so much that it creates drag.

## The Deepest Lesson: The Plan That Plans Itself

We asked: what does the "God Oracle" — the oracle above all oracles — advise?

The answer is mathematically beautiful: **the optimal plan is a fixed point.** It is the plan that, if you followed it, would cause you to create exactly the same plan again. It is self-consistent. It is its own justification.

This is not circular. It is the definition of optimality in dynamic programming (the Bellman equation). It means: the optimal plan doesn't fight reality. It incorporates reality's feedback. It is the plan you wouldn't change even after learning everything you'd learn by following it.

Most plans fail because they are not fixed points. You make a plan on Sunday night with fresh motivation. By Wednesday, reality has revealed information that makes the plan suboptimal. But instead of updating, you either doggedly follow the stale plan or abandon planning entirely.

The OGD framework says: **plan to re-plan.** Build the iteration into the system. Consult the Oracle Council regularly. Update the coupling matrix as you learn which goals actually help or hinder each other. Converge toward the fixed point.

Your goals are in orbit. And the laws of orbital mechanics are generous: they give you conservation laws to lean on, resonances to exploit, and gravity assists to ride. You don't need more willpower. You need better physics.
