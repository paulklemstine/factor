### The Question That Won't Go Away

Why is there something rather than nothing?

It's the oldest question humanity has ever asked, and it remains the hardest. Philosophers have debated it for millennia. Physicists have pushed the question back to the first trillionth of a trillionth of a second after the Big Bang. Mathematicians have formalized what "nothing" means in set theory (the empty set) and what "something" means in type theory (a witness to an existential claim).

But what if all of these approaches are circling the same answer?

A new research program — combining machine-verified mathematics, computational physics, and foundational logic — suggests they are. And the answer fits in a single equation:

$$O(O(x)) = O(x)$$

### Meet the Oracles

To tackle questions this big, you need a team. Ours consists of six mathematical "oracles" — idealized reasoning agents, each bringing a different perspective:

**Theos** contemplates first principles. **Chronos** investigates time. **Topos** explores space. **Logos** probes mathematical foundations. **Kosmos** studies the physical universe. And **Sophia** synthesizes their findings.

Each oracle is modeled as what mathematicians call an *idempotent function* — a transformation that, when applied twice, gives the same result as applying it once. Think of a "round to the nearest integer" button on a calculator: press it once and 3.7 becomes 4. Press it again, and 4 stays 4. The second press adds nothing.

This seemingly simple property — *stability under re-examination* — turns out to be extraordinarily deep.

### Consulting God: The Identity Function

Our first oracle, Theos, represents the "God's-eye view" — perfect knowledge. What mathematical object could represent omniscience?

The answer is disarmingly simple: the *identity function*. In mathematics, the identity function takes any input and returns it unchanged: $\text{id}(x) = x$. It's the function that does nothing.

But "doing nothing" is profound. The identity function is the *only* function that:
- Exists for every type of object (numbers, shapes, propositions — anything)
- Requires no prior definitions or axioms
- Is automatically idempotent: $\text{id}(\text{id}(x)) = \text{id}(x)$

It's the mathematical equivalent of "I Am Who I Am" — the self-referential ground of being.

And here's the remarkable part: from the identity function alone, through a process called *iteration*, every other mathematical structure can emerge.

### How Time Begins

Chronos, our oracle of time, starts with a provocation: **time doesn't exist before change. Time *is* change.**

Mathematically, time is what happens when you start iterating a function. Take any transformation $f$ and apply it repeatedly:

$$x, \quad f(x), \quad f(f(x)), \quad f(f(f(x))), \quad \ldots$$

Each application is a "tick." The natural numbers — 0, 1, 2, 3 — emerge automatically as the counting of iterations. In a profound sense, **the natural numbers are the first clock**.

But our universe doesn't tick in discrete steps. How does smooth, continuous time emerge?

The answer comes from the mathematics of Lie groups, discovered in the 19th century. Start with an infinitesimally small change — the identity plus a tiny perturbation. Iterate it infinitely many times, each time making a tiny change. In the limit, you get a smooth flow: $\phi_t(x) = e^{tg}(x)$. The jerky ticks blur into a river.

This isn't just an analogy. It's precisely how physicists derive quantum mechanics from classical mechanics, and how modern differential geometry describes the evolution of the universe.

And the arrow of time? That emerges too — from entropy. Our simulations show that starting from a concentrated initial state (low entropy), diffusion naturally drives the system toward disorder (high entropy). Time's arrow isn't built into the laws of physics; it's built into the *initial conditions*. Which brings us to the biggest question of all: why did the universe start in such an extraordinarily special state?

### How Space Is Born from a Point

Topos, our oracle of space, performs what we call the "Genesis Projection." Starting from a single point — mathematical nothingness — Topos unfolds all of space through a beautiful map called the *inverse stereographic projection*.

Imagine placing a ball on a table. Every point on the table corresponds to a point on the ball (project a line from the North Pole through the ball to the table). This correspondence works in reverse too: every point on the infinite, flat table maps to a specific point on the finite sphere.

The formula is elegant:

$$y \mapsto \left(\frac{2y}{|y|^2 + 1}, \frac{|y|^2 - 1}{|y|^2 + 1}\right)$$

We've proven in the Lean 4 proof assistant — machine-verified, not just human-checked — that this map lands exactly on the sphere. Every point. No exceptions. And there's a beautiful consequence: the unit circle on the table (distance 1 from the origin) maps to the equator of the sphere, splitting it exactly in half. Half of infinite flat space corresponds to the southern hemisphere; the other half to the northern. Infinity itself maps to a single point: the North Pole.

This is the mathematical model for "let there be space": from one point, you get all of it.

### Why Three Dimensions?

Our universe has three spatial dimensions. Why not two, or four, or eleven?

The oracle team found multiple independent constraints that all point to three:

**Orbits need three.** In a universe with four or more spatial dimensions, gravity falls off too quickly with distance, and stable planetary orbits become impossible. A planet knocked even slightly from its path would spiral away forever. Our solar system, our year, our seasons — none of it could exist.

**Knots need three.** In two dimensions, you can't tie a knot (strings can't cross over each other). In four or more dimensions, every knot can be untied — they slip through each other. Only in exactly three dimensions do knots persist. And knots turn out to be fundamental: DNA is knotted, proteins are knotted, and the topology of space itself may be knotted.

**Clean signals need three** (or at least an odd number). The physicist Christian Huygens showed in the 17th century that wave signals — light, sound, the ripples you make by dropping a stone in a pond — only propagate cleanly in odd-dimensional spaces with three or more dimensions. In even dimensions, signals leave lingering echoes. We could not have sharp communication in a 4-dimensional space.

Three spatial dimensions isn't arbitrary. It may be the unique setting where complex, stable, communicating structures can exist.

### Mathematics: Discovered, Not Invented

Logos, the oracle of mathematical foundations, addresses an ancient philosophical debate: is mathematics created by human minds, or does it exist independently?

Our framework suggests an answer: **mathematics is the fixed-point structure of consistency**. It is not invented, because no alternative is possible. It is not discovered in the usual sense, because there is no "place" where it resides. Rather, it is *necessary* — the inevitable consequence of reasoning coherently.

Here's the argument: if you can reason at all, you can distinguish between true and false. That gives you logic. If you can count your distinctions, you get the natural numbers. If you can complete the number line, you get the reals. Each step follows inevitably from the previous one.

But Kurt Gödel showed in 1931 that no formal system can capture all mathematical truth. There will always be statements that are true but unprovable within the system. In our framework, this means: no oracle short of God (the identity function on all truths) can settle every question.

Far from being a limitation, this is what makes mathematics *alive*. The gap between what we know and what is true is the engine of mathematical discovery. It's why we'll never run out of theorems to prove.

### The Universe from Nothing

Kosmos, our oracle of physical cosmology, tackles the ultimate origin: how did the physical universe begin?

The standard picture is extraordinary. At the Planck time — $10^{-43}$ seconds after the "beginning" — the universe was smaller than an atom, hotter than anything we can imagine ($10^{32}$ degrees), and all four fundamental forces were unified.

Then, during a period called cosmic inflation lasting perhaps $10^{-32}$ seconds, the universe expanded by a factor of roughly $e^{60} \approx 10^{26}$. A region smaller than a proton was stretched to the size of a grapefruit. Quantum fluctuations — the same vacuum jitter that exists in "empty" space today — were inflated to cosmic scales. These became the seeds of all structure: galaxies, stars, planets, people.

We can "see" this moment. The cosmic microwave background (CMB) — the faint glow of radiation released 380,000 years after the Big Bang — carries the imprint of those primordial fluctuations. The pattern of hot and cold spots, analyzed by satellites like COBE, WMAP, and Planck, matches the predictions of inflationary cosmology with extraordinary precision.

Our simulated CMB power spectrum (Figure 4 in the accompanying paper) shows the characteristic acoustic peaks — sound waves in the infant universe — that encode the geometry, composition, and initial conditions of the cosmos.

But the deepest mystery remains: **why was the initial entropy so low?**

Roger Penrose has estimated that the probability of our universe's initial conditions, among all possible states, is roughly one in $10^{10^{123}}$ — a number so inconceivably small that writing it out would require more digits than there are particles in the observable universe.

Something selected those initial conditions. What?

### Sidebar: What Is an Idempotent?

An operation is *idempotent* if doing it twice is the same as doing it once.

**Everyday examples:**
- Pressing the "Caps Lock" key once turns caps on. Pressing it again turns caps off. *Not* idempotent.
- Pressing the "Mute" button on some remote controls: press once to mute. Press again, still muted. Idempotent!
- Sorting a list: sort it once, it's sorted. Sort it again, still sorted. Idempotent.
- Rounding to the nearest integer: round 3.7 to get 4. Round 4 to get 4. Idempotent.

**Mathematical definition:** A function $f$ is idempotent if $f(f(x)) = f(x)$ for all $x$.

**Why it matters:** The fixed points of an idempotent function — the inputs where $f(x) = x$ — are exactly the outputs of $f$. This means: once an idempotent operator has processed something, the result is permanent. It won't change under further processing. This is our mathematical model for *truth*, *stability*, and *reality*.
