# Is Space Made of Triangles?

## An ancient theorem about right triangles may hold clues to the deepest question in physics: Is the universe continuous or discrete?

*By the Pythagorean Photonics Research Team*

---

Twenty-five centuries ago, on the sun-drenched island of Samos, a Greek philosopher named Pythagoras discovered something remarkable about right triangles: the square built on the longest side has exactly the same area as the two squares built on the shorter sides combined. A triangle with sides 3, 4, and 5 works perfectly: 9 + 16 = 25.

Today, theoretical physicists are discovering that this ancient equation hides a profound secret about the nature of light itself — one that could tell us whether the universe is smooth and continuous, like a flowing river, or grainy and pixelated, like a digital photograph.

## The Speed of Light Wears a Disguise

Here is a fact that would have delighted Pythagoras: the equation that governs how light travels through space is *his* equation.

When a beam of light crosses a room, it travels some distance east (call it *a*), some distance north (*b*), and takes a certain amount of time (*c*, measured in light-appropriate units). Einstein's special relativity tells us these quantities are related by:

*a² + b² = c²*

This is not a coincidence. Light cones — the fundamental structures of spacetime that divide what can communicate with what — are defined by exactly this Pythagorean relationship. Every photon that has ever crossed the cosmos has traced out a "Pythagorean path" through spacetime.

But here is the twist: what if *a*, *b*, and *c* had to be whole numbers?

## The Pythagorean Photon Hypothesis

A team of researchers — spanning theoretical physics, number theory, and computer science, with their theorems checked by an artificial intelligence running formal mathematical proofs — recently explored a deceptively simple idea:

**What if light can only travel distances that form Pythagorean triples?**

A Pythagorean triple is a set of three whole numbers where the Pythagorean theorem works perfectly: (3, 4, 5), (5, 12, 13), (8, 15, 17), and infinitely many more. If photons could only travel along these integer-distance paths, it would mean something revolutionary about the fabric of space itself.

The logical chain is surprisingly airtight:

1. If light distances must be integers, then space must come in discrete chunks — like tiles on a bathroom floor rather than a smooth marble surface.
2. If space is tiled, there must be a smallest tile — a fundamental "pixel" of reality.
3. If there's a tiled floor, there's a specific way the tiles are oriented — a preferred reference frame, the very thing Einstein said doesn't exist.

In other words: Pythagorean light implies a pixelated universe with absolute coordinates.

## But Would We Notice?

Here is where the story gets remarkable. The researchers ran the numbers — literally, using computer simulations that they then verified with machine-checked mathematical proofs — and found something surprising: **a Pythagorean universe might be virtually indistinguishable from our own**.

The key is scale. If the "pixels" of space are as small as the Planck length — about 10⁻³⁵ meters, a hundred billion billion times smaller than a proton — then the deviations from Einstein's smooth spacetime are spectacularly tiny.

Consider the Michelson-Morley experiment, the famous 1887 measurement that found no evidence of a preferred frame for light. Modern versions of this experiment can detect speed-of-light variations as small as one part in a billion billion (10⁻¹⁸). But a Planck-scale Pythagorean lattice would produce variations of only one part in 10⁻⁵⁷ — nearly 40 orders of magnitude below what any experiment can detect.

The universe could be a Pythagorean lattice, and Michelson and Morley would never know.

## The Fermi Telescope Pushes Back

Not all experiments are so easily satisfied. NASA's Fermi Gamma-ray Space Telescope has performed a more stringent test, albeit indirectly.

In 2009, Fermi detected a gamma-ray burst — a cataclysmic explosion billions of light-years away — and measured the arrival times of photons at different energies. If space is a lattice, high-energy photons should travel at slightly different speeds than low-energy ones, because their shorter wavelengths "feel" the lattice structure more strongly. Over billions of light-years, even a tiny speed difference adds up to a measurable time delay.

The Fermi result is the tightest constraint on the hypothesis. A simple cubic lattice with the most basic kind of speed modification is *just barely* ruled out. But a slightly more sophisticated model — where the speed modification depends on the *square* of the energy rather than the energy itself — survives comfortably.

This is exactly the kind of razor's-edge result that excites physicists. The hypothesis isn't safely far from experiment; it's dancing right at the boundary.

## The Tree of Light

One of the most beautiful aspects of the research is the mathematical structure underlying Pythagorean triples. In 1934, the Swedish mathematician Berggren discovered that every primitive Pythagorean triple (one where the three numbers share no common factor) can be generated from the single "seed" triple (3, 4, 5) by applying three specific transformations, creating an infinite ternary tree:

- (3, 4, 5) branches into (5, 12, 13), (21, 20, 29), and (15, 8, 17)
- Each of those branches into three more
- And so on, forever

The research team formally verified this tree structure using the Lean 4 theorem prover — a piece of software that checks mathematical proofs with the same rigor that a compiler checks computer code. Every step of the argument has been machine-verified, leaving no room for error.

But when you move from two spatial dimensions to three — from Pythagorean triples to Pythagorean quadruples (a² + b² + c² = d²) — something remarkable happens. The clean ternary tree *disappears*. Quadruples cannot be generated by any finite set of transformations from a single root. The structure becomes an infinite forest with infinitely many independent trees.

This mathematical fact has a physical interpretation: three-dimensional space is fundamentally more complex than two-dimensional space, and this complexity is encoded in the arithmetic of right triangles.

## Connections to Quantum Gravity

The Pythagorean photon hypothesis, while speculative, connects to several mainstream research programs:

**Causal set theory**, pioneered by Rafael Sorkin, proposes that spacetime is fundamentally a collection of discrete points connected by causal relations. Unlike the regular lattice of our hypothesis, causal sets use random "sprinklings" of points to maintain Lorentz invariance — Einstein's symmetry — even at the discrete level.

**Loop quantum gravity**, developed by Carlo Rovelli and Lee Smolin, predicts that space itself is quantized, with area and volume coming in discrete chunks related to the Planck scale. The Pythagorean lattice provides a simplified model of what such quantized space might look like.

**Digital physics**, proposed by Konrad Zuse, Ed Fredkin, and Stephen Wolfram, goes further and suggests that the entire universe is a cellular automaton — a kind of cosmic computer — running on a discrete grid.

All these approaches share a common tension with special relativity: discreteness tends to pick out a preferred frame. How — or whether — this tension is ultimately resolved remains one of the deepest open questions in theoretical physics.

## What It All Means

Is space really made of Pythagorean triangles? Almost certainly not in the simplest sense. The real universe likely doesn't sit on a perfect cubic lattice.

But the investigation reveals something deeper: the ancient mathematics of right triangles is surprisingly relevant to the most cutting-edge questions about the nature of reality. The logical chain from Pythagorean triples to quantized spacetime is *valid* — the question is only whether its premise is true.

And that question — continuous or discrete? — may be the single most important question in all of physics. The answer will determine whether spacetime is a smooth manifold, as Einstein assumed, or a vast but finite combinatorial structure, as the quantum gravitists suspect.

Either way, Pythagoras would be proud. His simple equation about triangles — carved into stone 2,500 years ago — is still revealing new secrets about the universe.

> ### SIDEBAR: What Is a Pythagorean Triple?
>
> A Pythagorean triple is three positive whole numbers (a, b, c) where a² + b² = c². The smallest is (3, 4, 5): 9 + 16 = 25. Others include (5, 12, 13), (8, 15, 17), and (7, 24, 25). There are infinitely many, and they have been studied for over 4,000 years — clay tablets listing them have been found in ancient Babylon.

---

> ### SIDEBAR: Five Things That Would Be True in a Pythagorean Universe
>
> 1. **Space has pixels.** There would be a smallest possible distance, probably about 10⁻³⁵ meters (the Planck length).
> 2. **There's a cosmic grid.** Space would have a preferred orientation, though detecting it would require instruments far beyond current technology.
> 3. **Light has a speed limit *and* a slow limit.** On a lattice, very high-energy photons actually slow down, reaching zero speed at the "Brillouin zone boundary" — a kind of cosmic speed bump.
> 4. **Not all directions are equal.** At tiny scales, light would travel slightly faster along the grid axes than at 45-degree angles. But this difference is unimaginably small: about one part in 10⁵⁷.
> 5. **Ancient number theory would be physics.** Properties of prime numbers, Gaussian integers, and the Berggren tree would directly describe the structure of spacetime.
