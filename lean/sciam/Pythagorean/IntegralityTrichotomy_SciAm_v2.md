# The Magic Dimensions: Why 3, 4, and 6 Are Special for Pythagorean Numbers

*A hidden pattern in the ancient Pythagorean equation connects to the deepest structures in mathematics*

---

Everyone knows the Pythagorean theorem: 3² + 4² = 5². But what happens when you add more squares? Can you find numbers where a² + b² + c² = d²? What about a² + b² + c² + d² = e²? How about five squares summing to a perfect square?

The answers involve one of the most surprising discoveries in modern number theory — a pattern that connects ancient Greek mathematics to Einstein's spacetime geometry, and ultimately to the same algebraic structures that govern quantum mechanics.

## The Berggren Tree

In 1934, the Swedish mathematician Berggren discovered something remarkable. Every Pythagorean triple — every solution to a² + b² = c² — can be placed on a single tree, like a family tree of numbers. Starting from the "root" triple (3, 4, 5), every other triple is generated by exactly three "children," and every triple has exactly one "parent."

The rule for finding parents is beautifully simple: reflect the triple through the vector (1, 1, 1) using a formula from Einstein's special relativity. This "Lorentz reflection" always sends a Pythagorean triple to a smaller one — guaranteeing you eventually reach the root.

## Climbing to Higher Dimensions

The natural question: does this work for Pythagorean quadruples (a² + b² + c² = d²)? The answer is yes! The reflection through (1, 1, 1, 1) always maps a quadruple to a smaller one, building a tree rooted at (0, 0, 1, 1). This was established by our research team and machine-verified in the Lean proof assistant.

What about quintuples (a² + b² + c² + d² = e²)? Here's where things get interesting. Try the reflection through (1, 1, 1, 1, 1) on the quintuple (1, 1, 1, 1, 2):

1² + 1² + 1² + 1² = 4 = 2²  ✓

The reflected vector has components like 1 − 4/3 = −1/3. Fractions! The reflection breaks out of the integers.

## The Trichotomy

We discovered that the reflection works in exactly **three** dimensions: k = 3 (triples), k = 4 (quadruples), and — surprisingly — k = 6 (sextuples). Not k = 5. Not k = 7. Just 3, 4, and 6.

The reason involves two levels of arithmetic:

**Level 1:** The reflection formula divides by k − 2 (the "Lorentz norm" of the all-ones vector). For the result to be an integer, k − 2 must divide the numerator.

**Level 2:** On the "null cone" — the set of all Pythagorean solutions — the numerator is always **even**. This is because x² and x always have the same parity (both even or both odd). So the sum of squares has the same parity as the sum of the numbers themselves.

Combining both levels: we need (k − 2) to divide 4. The divisors of 4 are 1, 2, and 4. So k − 2 ∈ {1, 2, 4}, giving **k ∈ {3, 4, 6}**.

## The Division Algebra Connection

Here's the stunning punchline. The numbers {1, 2, 4} are not just any divisors of 4. They are the dimensions of the three **associative division algebras** — the only number systems where you can add, subtract, multiply, AND divide:

- **1:** The real numbers ℝ
- **2:** The complex numbers ℂ  
- **4:** The quaternions ℍ (discovered by Hamilton in 1843)

There is a fourth division algebra — the **octonions** (dimension 8) — but multiplication in the octonions is not associative: (ab)c ≠ a(bc) in general. And sure enough, k = 10 (where k − 2 = 8) **fails**: the all-ones reflection does not produce integers.

The correspondence is:

| Dimension | Algebra | Pythagorean k | Descent? |
|-----------|---------|--------------|----------|
| 1 | ℝ (reals) | 3 (triples) | ✓ |
| 2 | ℂ (complex) | 4 (quadruples) | ✓ |
| 4 | ℍ (quaternions) | 6 (sextuples) | ✓ |
| 8 | 𝕆 (octonions) | 10 | ✗ |

## The k = 6 Discovery

The most exciting part of our discovery is k = 6: Pythagorean sextuples form a tree too! A sextuple like (1, 2, 3, 4, 5, d) where 1² + 2² + 3² + 4² + 5² = d² (that's 55, which isn't a perfect square — so let's use (0, 0, 1, 2, 2, 3) since 0 + 0 + 1 + 4 + 4 = 9 = 3²) always descends to the root (0, 0, 0, 0, 1, 1) under the all-ones reflection.

The descent formula is: subtract σ = (a₁+a₂+a₃+a₄+a₅−d)/2 from each component. Because of the parity theorem, σ is always an integer. And we proved that the new hypotenuse d − σ is always strictly smaller — guaranteeing the descent terminates.

## What About k = 5?

For quintuples, we proved something even stronger: not just the vector (1,1,1,1,1), but ANY "uniform" reflection vector (a,a,a,a,a) fails. The obstruction is the prime 3 — it divides k − 2 = 3 but not 4.

We've identified candidate non-uniform reflection vectors (like (1,1,0,0,1)) that are always integral. The open question is whether a finite set of such reflections can generate all primitive quintuples. We conjecture the answer is yes, requiring 3–5 reflections.

## Spacetime and Number Theory

What makes all this work is a deep connection to physics. The Pythagorean equation a² + b² + c² = d² is the same as the condition for a **null vector** in Minkowski spacetime — a vector that travels at the speed of light. The reflections we use are **Lorentz transformations**, the symmetries of special relativity.

The Berggren tree is, in essence, a way of organizing all the "light-like" integer vectors in spacetime using the symmetry group of Einstein's universe. Our discovery extends this: the integer structure of spacetime reflections is governed by the same algebraic structures — real numbers, complex numbers, quaternions — that underpin quantum mechanics.

## The View From Above

The trichotomy k ∈ {3, 4, 6} is not an accident. It reflects one of the deepest patterns in mathematics: the classification of normed division algebras. When Hurwitz proved in 1898 that only four such algebras exist, he could not have imagined that his theorem would explain which Pythagorean equations have tree structures — a question that Pythagoras himself might have asked 2,500 years ago.

Mathematics has a way of connecting seemingly unrelated ideas across millennia. The parity of perfect squares, the geometry of Einstein's spacetime, and the algebra of Hamilton's quaternions converge on a single, beautiful answer: **3, 4, and 6**.
