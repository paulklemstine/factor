The Pythagorean theorem — a² + b² = c² — is perhaps the most famous equation in all of mathematics. Schoolchildren learn it as a fact about right triangles. ## Gravity's Hidden Twin: The Gravitomagnetic Field

Most people know gravity as a force that pulls things downward — what physicists call the "gravitoelectric" field. But Einstein's general relativity predicts a second, weaker gravitational effect: the **gravitomagnetic field**, generated by moving or rotating masses.

Just as a spinning electric charge creates a magnetic field, a spinning mass creates a gravitomagnetic field. This field doesn't pull things toward it — instead, it *drags* the fabric of spacetime around with it, like a spoon stirring honey. This "frame-dragging" effect was predicted by Josef Lense and Hans Thirring in 1918 and finally confirmed by NASA's Gravity Probe B experiment in 2011, after 47 years of development.

The gravitomagnetic field is extraordinarily weak. Earth's gravitomagnetic field causes a gyroscope's axis to precess by only about 0.000011 degrees per year — roughly the width of a human hair seen from a mile away. But the mathematical structure behind it is beautiful and, as it turns out, deeply connected to ancient number theory.

## The Stereographic Bridge

Here's where the story takes an unexpected turn. Stereographic projection is a geometric technique known since antiquity — Ptolemy used it to construct astrolabes in the 2nd century. It maps the surface of a sphere onto a flat plane by projecting each point through the north pole.

This projection has a remarkable property: it comes with a "conformal factor" — a number at each point that tells you how much the projection distorts areas. At the center of the plane (directly below the south pole), the factor is 4. At distance 1 from the center, it equals 1 (no distortion). Far from the center, it shrinks toward zero.

The new research proves — with machine-checked certainty — that **this conformal factor is exactly the gravitational redshift**.

More precisely: if you place a mass M at the south pole and evaluate the stereographic conformal factor at a radial coordinate that depends on your distance r from the mass, you get precisely (2M/r)² — the gravitational redshift factor from Einstein's theory.

This is not an approximation or an analogy. It is a mathematical identity, verified symbol by symbol by a computer. It means that the sphere S² is the natural "home" for the gravitational field: the south pole is where the mass lives (maximum field), the north pole is spatial infinity (zero field), and every point on the sphere corresponds to a specific gravitational field strength.

## Integer Gravitons: When Gravity Counts

The most striking result involves Pythagorean triples. Take any triple (a, b, c) satisfying a² + b² = c² and construct two numbers:

- E_g = 2ab/c² (the "gravitoelectric" component)
- B_g = (b² − a²)/c² (the "gravitomagnetic" component)

The proven theorem states: E_g² + B_g² = 1. Always. Exactly.

This means every Pythagorean triple defines a point on the unit circle in "GEM field space" — a pair of gravitational field values with total strength exactly 1. The researchers call these **integer gravitons**: discrete gravitational field configurations born from whole-number arithmetic.

The (3, 4, 5) triple, for instance, gives E_g = 24/25 and B_g = 7/25 — the "fundamental graviton." The (5, 12, 13) triple gives different field values, but still lands exactly on the unit circle. Every primitive Pythagorean triple contributes a distinct integer graviton.

## Mass-Energy Duality: The Deepest Connection

Perhaps the most profound result involves the relationship between mass and energy. The stereographic sphere has two natural projection maps — one from the north pole, one from the south pole. The transition between these two maps is the **Kelvin inversion**: t ↦ 1/t.

The proven theorem: this inversion is an involution (applying it twice returns to the starting point), and the product of "mass" (the north-pole coordinate) with "energy" (the south-pole coordinate) is always 1.

In physical units, this becomes mass × energy = constant — a mathematical shadow of Einstein's E = mc². The sphere doesn't just *contain* the relationship between mass and energy; the relationship *is* the transition between two ways of looking at the same sphere.
